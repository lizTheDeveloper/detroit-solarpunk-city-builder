# Detroit Solarpunk City Builder: Historical & Contextual Research

This reference document provides real-world Detroit history, ecology, politics, and cultural context to inform authentic game content. Every fact includes a source citation.

---

## 1. Detroit Neighborhoods (for the Tile Map)

### 1.1 Downtown

- **Location**: Central Business District along the Detroit River, anchored by Campus Martius Park
- **Character/Identity**: Corporate center, sports venues (Comerica Park, Ford Field, Little Caesars Arena), and the symbolic heart of Detroit's revival narrative. Heavy Dan Gilbert/Bedrock presence.
- **Current State**: 54 tracked development projects representing over $2.7 billion in investment. Residential units saw a 14% increase in 2024 with 500 new units completed. Hotel and residential projects comprise 2/3 of developments under construction. [Source: Downtown Detroit Partnership, "2024 Downtown Detroit Community and Development Update," https://downtowndetroit.org/news-insights/the-2024-downtown-detroit-update/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Renaissance Center (GM HQ), Campus Martius, Spirit of Detroit statue, Cobo Center (now Huntington Place), Detroit RiverWalk
- **Relevant History**: Site of original French settlement (Fort Pontchartrain du Detroit, 1701). Devastated by 1805 fire, rebuilt on radial street plan by Augustus Woodward.
- **Community Organizations**: Downtown Detroit Partnership
- **Game Transformation**: Tension between corporate-led revival and community access. Projects could address affordable housing, public transit, and converting surface parking lots to community spaces.

### 1.2 Midtown / Cultural Center

- **Location**: North of Downtown along Woodward Avenue, centered on Wayne State University
- **Character/Identity**: Detroit's cultural and institutional hub. Home to world-class museums, a major university, and the city's densest walkable neighborhood.
- **Current State**: The $377 million "Mid Project" began construction in early 2025, featuring a 15-story hotel, apartment complexes, and retail. Rapidly gentrifying. [Source: Hour Detroit, "Downtown Detroit Projects Opening in 2026 and Beyond," https://www.hourdetroit.com/development-topics/downtown-detroit-projects-opening-2026-beyond/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Detroit Institute of Arts, Charles H. Wright Museum of African American History, Detroit Historical Museum, Detroit Public Library (main branch), Wayne State University, Detroit Science Center [Source: Wikipedia, "Cultural Center Historic District (Detroit)," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_Center_Historic_District_(Detroit,_Michigan)]
- **Relevant History**: Cultural Center district established in early 20th century. DIA houses Diego Rivera's "Detroit Industry" murals (1932-33), a massive fresco cycle celebrating and critiquing industrial labor.
- **Community Organizations**: Midtown Detroit Inc., University Cultural Center Association
- **Game Transformation**: Education and cultural programming hub. Tension between institutional expansion and displacement of longtime residents.

### 1.3 Corktown

- **Location**: West of Downtown, south of Michigan Avenue
- **Character/Identity**: Detroit's oldest surviving neighborhood, originally settled by Irish immigrants from County Cork in the 1830s-1840s. Now a trendy hub of restaurants, bars, and Ford's new campus. [Source: Detroit Historical Society, "Corktown Historic District," https://www.detroithistorical.org/learn/online-research/encyclopedia-of-detroit/corktown-historic-district]
- **Current State**: Rapid gentrification since Ford's 2018 purchase of Michigan Central Station. Median sale prices jumped 14% in under a year after the Ford announcement. [Source: Deadline Detroit, "Unease in Detroit's Historic Corktown," https://www.deadlinedetroit.com/articles/22498/unease_in_detroit_s_historic_corktown_not_every_neighborhood_wants_to_be_midtown]
- **Key Landmarks**: Michigan Central Station (Ford's $740 million campus), Roosevelt Park, Tiger Stadium site (now residential), Mercury Bar, Slow's Bar BQ
- **Relevant History**: 75 acres demolished in mid-20th century for industrial expansion that never came. The Lodge Freeway and "urban renewal" destroyed dozens of residential blocks in the 1960s. [Source: Segregation by Design, "Corktown," https://www.segregationbydesign.com/detroit/corktown]
- **Community Organizations**: Corktown Neighborhood Association, Greater Corktown Development Corporation
- **Game Transformation**: Gentrification battleground. Projects could focus on anti-displacement policies, community land trusts, and ensuring longtime residents benefit from Ford campus investment.

### 1.4 Brightmoor

- **Location**: Far northwest Detroit, near the Redford Township border
- **Character/Identity**: One of Detroit's most challenged neighborhoods, but also one of the most organized. Originally a planned community of inexpensive housing for workers.
- **Current State**: Housing vacancy rate of 29.4% (compared to citywide 22.8%). Population declined from 26,040 in 1970 to approximately 9,372. [Source: Data Driven Detroit, "Brightmoor Community Needs Assessment," https://datadrivendetroit.org/files/BCNA/D3_BrightmoorNeedsAssessment.pdf]
- **Key Landmarks**: Brightmoor Community Center, Eliza Howell Park
- **Relevant History**: Created in 1922 by developer B.E. Taylor, who recruited workers from Appalachia for auto plants. Annexed by Detroit in 1926. Crime surges in the 1990s-2000s drove further population loss. [Source: Wikipedia, "Brightmoor, Detroit," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brightmoor,_Detroit]
- **Community Organizations**: Brightmoor Alliance (coalition formed 2000), Motor City Blight Busters, Neighbors Building Brightmoor, Brightmoor Artisans Collective. In June 2013, the Detroit Blight Authority cleaned a 14-block area. [Source: Brightmoor Alliance, "History of Brightmoor," https://brightmooralliance.wordpress.com/resources/history-of-brightmoor/]
- **Game Transformation**: Ideal for urban agriculture, community solar, land stewardship. Extensive vacant land allows transformative projects. High vacancy = high potential for food forests and ecological restoration.

### 1.5 Mexicantown / Southwest Detroit

- **Location**: Southwest Detroit, centered around Bagley Avenue, Vernor Highway, and West Vernor
- **Character/Identity**: Detroit's most vibrant immigrant neighborhood. Predominantly Mexican and Latino, but also home to Filipino, Vietnamese, and various European immigrant communities. Known for murals, family restaurants, and the annual Cinco de Mayo parade. [Source: Visit Detroit, "Southwest Detroit: Preserving and Making History," https://visitdetroit.com/inside-the-d/southwest-detroit-neighborhood/]
- **Current State**: One of the few Detroit neighborhoods that maintained population during the city's decline. Strong entrepreneurial culture and cultural identity. Faces affordability pressures from recent development. [Source: LISC, "The Enduring Heritage of Detroit's Mexicantown," https://www.lisc.org/our-stories/story/enduringand-enterprisingheritage-detroits-mexicantown/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Clark Park, Mexicantown Welcome Center, Holy Redeemer Church, numerous murals (rooted in Chicano mural movement of 1960s-70s), Ambassador Bridge
- **Relevant History**: Mexican immigrants arrived in the 1920s; WWII brought waves of farmworkers. The area was dubbed "Mexicantown" in the late 1980s. Murals have deep cultural roots stretching to pre-Hispanic Mexico. [Source: National Trust for Historic Preservation, "Next Stop, Mexicantown," https://savingplaces.org/stories/next-stop-mexicantown-a-historic-detroit-neighborhood-prepares-for-change]
- **Community Organizations**: Congress of Communities, Southwest Detroit Business Association, Latin Americans for Social and Economic Development (LA SED)
- **Game Transformation**: Cultural preservation, environmental justice (adjacent to industrial pollution sources), community-controlled development. Cultural murals as narrative/art mechanic.

### 1.6 Eastern Market

- **Location**: Just northeast of Downtown, along Gratiot Avenue
- **Character/Identity**: One of the oldest and largest continuously operating public markets in the US, dating to 1891. A food hub connecting urban and regional food systems. [Source: Eastern Market Corporation, https://easternmarket.org/]
- **Current State**: Evolving from a traditional farmers' market into a regional food hub supporting small local growers, food processors, and creative retail. Over 150 businesses in the district. [Source: Metromode, "Eastern Market: A Food Hub for Metro Detroit and Beyond," https://metromodemedia.com/metrodetroitfoodhub0241/]
- **Key Landmarks**: The iconic market sheds (built 1920s-30s with distinctive arched roofs), Russell Street corridor, Shed 5
- **Relevant History**: Originally opened in Cadillac Square in 1841, relocated to current location in 1891. Started as a hay and wood market. During the Great Depression, it offered direct-from-producer food at lower prices. After WWII, wholesalers and food processors concentrated in the area. [Source: Ordo, "A Brief History of Eastern Market," https://www.ordo.com/articles/a-brief-history-of-eastern-market-detroits-favorite-farmers-market]
- **Community Organizations**: Eastern Market Corporation, community gardens, food education programs
- **Game Transformation**: Natural hub for food sovereignty projects. Expanding the market model to a citywide network of food hubs. Connection point between urban farms and regional food systems.

### 1.7 Belle Isle

- **Location**: 982-acre island in the Detroit River, connected by the MacArthur Bridge
- **Character/Identity**: Detroit's jewel park, designed by Frederick Law Olmsted Sr. in 1881. A natural refuge in international waters between the US and Canada.
- **Current State**: Became a Michigan State Park in 2013 under a 30-year lease, saving Detroit millions in maintenance costs. Some controversy over state control and vehicle entry fees. [Source: Belle Isle Conservancy, https://www.belleisleconservancy.org/about-belle-isle]
- **Key Landmarks**: Anna Scripps Whitcomb Conservatory, Dossin Great Lakes Museum, Belle Isle Aquarium (oldest in US), James Scott Memorial Fountain, Giant Slide, historic casino building
- **Relevant History**: Indigenous peoples called it Mahnahbezee ("the swan"). French colonists named it Ile aux Cochons (Hog Island). Renamed Belle Isle on July 4, 1845. Purchased by Detroit for $200,000 in 1879. Almost one-third is natural wooded area. [Source: Detroit Historical Society, "Belle Isle Park," https://www.detroithistorical.org/learn/online-research/encyclopedia-of-detroit/belle-isle-park]
- **Ecology**: Migratory flyway for waterfowl. Home to raccoons, opossums, Great Horned Owls, foxes, squirrels, beaver, and a wide variety of birds. [Source: Michigan Sea Grant, "Beautiful Belle Isle," https://www.canr.msu.edu/news/beautiful-belle-isle-detroit-s-unique-urban-park-msg18-bohling18]
- **Game Transformation**: Ecological restoration showcase. Wildlife corridor, wetland restoration, native plant gardens. Tension between state park model and community access.

### 1.8 Indian Village

- **Location**: East side, between Jefferson and Mack Avenues along Burns, Iroquois, and Seminole Streets
- **Character/Identity**: One of Detroit's most architecturally significant neighborhoods. Grand historic homes built for Detroit's elite, including automotive entrepreneurs like Edsel Ford. [Source: Historic Indian Village, https://historicindianvillage.org/]
- **Current State**: Well-preserved historic district (designated 1971). Active community organizations maintain the neighborhood's character. Over 350 homes with 17 architectural styles built between 1895 and the late 1920s. [Source: Wikipedia, "Indian Village, Detroit," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Village,_Detroit]
- **Key Landmarks**: Homes designed by Albert Kahn, Louis Kamper, William B. Stratton. Some homes over 12,000 sq ft.
- **Relevant History**: Conceived as a "first-class residential district on a generous scale." Attracted Detroit's most prosperous families, including automotive industry leaders.
- **Community Organizations**: Indian Village Association, Men's Garden Club, Women's Garden Club. Annual Home & Garden Tour (first Saturday in June).
- **Game Transformation**: Historic preservation model. Community heritage projects. Contrast point showing how wealth and architectural investment can maintain neighborhoods.

### 1.9 Delray

- **Location**: Southwest Detroit, between Fort Street and the Detroit River, from the Ambassador Bridge south to Zug Island
- **Character/Identity**: Ground zero for environmental justice in Detroit. Surrounded by heavy industry, including Marathon Oil's refinery. Predominantly low-income, ~50% people of color. [Source: U of M Environmental Justice Case Study, https://websites.umich.edu/~snre492/Jones/delray.htm]
- **Current State**: Located in ZIP code 48217, considered one of the most polluted ZIP codes in Michigan and the US. Marathon Petroleum's refinery produces up to 140,000 barrels of oil daily, 24/7. In 2014, the refinery emitted 430 tons of nitrous oxide, 83 tons of PM2.5, and 211 tons of sulfur dioxide. Residents have asthma hospitalization rates three times the state average. [Source: Harvard Medical School, "Air Quality in Southwest Detroit," https://info.primarycare.hms.harvard.edu/perspectives/articles/air-quality-southwest-detroit]
- **Key Landmarks**: Marathon Oil Refinery, Zug Island (US Steel), Ambassador Bridge, proposed Gordie Howe International Bridge
- **Relevant History**: Once a thriving working-class neighborhood. Decades of industrial waste discharge created extensive air and soil pollution. Approximately 80% of residents are Black, 12% Hispanic, and over 40% live in poverty. [Source: PGE Project, "Marathon Oil Refinery (Detroit)," https://pgeproject.wordpress.com/2021/04/20/marathon-oil-refinery-detroit-michigan/]
- **Community Organizations**: Southwest Detroit Environmental Vision, Delray Community Council
- **Game Transformation**: Quintessential environmental justice tile. Soil remediation, air quality monitoring, industrial transition, resident relocation/support, brownfield cleanup. The hardest transformation in the game.

### 1.10 Hamtramck (Enclave City)

- **Location**: 2.1-square-mile enclave surrounded by Detroit, about 5 miles north of downtown
- **Character/Identity**: The most densely populated municipality in Michigan. Transformed from "Little Warsaw" (90% Polish in 1970) to the only Muslim-majority city in the United States. Home to Yemeni, Bangladeshi, Bosnian, and other immigrant communities. [Source: Wikipedia, "Hamtramck, Michigan," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamtramck,_Michigan]
- **Current State**: Population 28,433 (2020 census). In 2022, Hamtramck became the first city in the US with a fully Muslim city council. [Source: Context Corner, "How Hamtramck Became America's First Islamic-Led City," https://context-corner.com/news/how-hamtramck-became-americas-first/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Hamtramck Disneyland (folk art installation), Mu'ath bin Jabal Mosque (est. 1976), Joseph Campau main street, Hamtramck Stadium (Negro Leagues site)
- **Relevant History**: Originally a Polish immigrant community; younger, wealthier Polish Americans left for suburbs, creating affordable housing that attracted new immigrant waves. Bangladeshi community from the 1930s, Yemeni from the 1960s.
- **Community Organizations**: One New Humanity CDC, Hamtramck Community Initiative
- **Game Transformation**: Immigrant integration, cultural preservation, interfaith dialogue, affordable housing. Technically an independent city -- represents intergovernmental cooperation challenges and opportunities.

### 1.11 Highland Park (Enclave City)

- **Location**: Independent city enclosed by Detroit, roughly 6 miles north of Downtown
- **Character/Identity**: The birthplace of the moving assembly line. Now one of the poorest communities in Michigan, with a dramatic streetlight crisis that inspired grassroots solar solutions. [Source: Next City, "The Utility Repossessed Their City's Streetlights," https://nextcity.org/urbanist-news/utility-repossessed-citys-streetlights-now-theyre-relighting-the-night]
- **Current State**: 92% Black, 47% below the poverty line, median household income ~$18,000. Many residents spend 10-30% of income on utilities. More than two-thirds of residential streetlights were removed in 2011 when the city owed DTE Energy $4 million. [Source: HuffPost, "Highland Park Lighting Crisis Spurs Citizens to Action," https://www.huffpost.com/entry/highland-park-lighting-solar-streetlight_n_2172782]
- **Key Landmarks**: Highland Park Ford Plant (1909, first assembly line 1913), Model T Plaza
- **Relevant History**: Population boomed from ~4,000 to 46,500 between 1910-1920 during the auto boom. Ford demolished much of its plant in the late 1950s. Incorporated as a city in 1918 to protect its tax base from Detroit's expanding boundaries. [Source: Wikipedia, "Highland Park, Michigan," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highland_Park,_Michigan]
- **Community Organizations**: Soulardarity -- formed to restore streetlighting with solar-powered lights. A 501(c)(3) that has become a national model for community-owned renewable energy. [Source: Michigan Public, "Highland Park residents lighting up their streets with solar power," https://www.michiganpublic.org/environment-science/2017-02-21/highland-park-residents-lighting-up-their-streets-with-solar-power]
- **Game Transformation**: THE solarpunk origin story in the game. Community-owned solar, energy democracy, utility independence. Soulardarity is a real-world model for game projects.

### 1.12 Palmer Park

- **Location**: Northwest Detroit, off McNichols Road and Woodward Avenue
- **Character/Identity**: Historically Detroit's LGBTQ neighborhood ("gayborhood"). Now a hub for the Black LGBTQ community. Over 50 apartment buildings gave it a unique density and attracted diverse residents.
- **Current State**: Hosts "Hotter Than July," Detroit's Black LGBTQ celebration attracting thousands from across the Midwest. [Source: WDET, "CuriosiD: Did Detroit Ever Have A Gayborhood?," https://wdet.org/2016/07/11/curiosid-did-detroit-ever-have-a-gayborhood/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Palmer Park (the park itself), historic apartment buildings ("The Barracks" -- one building had 46 of 48 units occupied by gay men in the 1980s)
- **Relevant History**: In the late 1960s-80s, a network of eight bars, two gay-friendly bookstores, and restaurants created a vibrant LGBTQ district. By the 1990s, many white LGBTQ residents moved to suburban Royal Oak and Ferndale. [Source: Model D, "HISTORY LESSON: The significance of the Palmer Park Historic District," https://modeldmedia.com/history-lesson-the-significance-of-the-palmer-park-historic-district/]
- **Community Organizations**: Palmer Park neighborhood association, LGBTQ organizations
- **Game Transformation**: LGBTQ cultural preservation, community center projects, affordable housing for diverse communities. Unique cultural identity that should be reflected in neighborhood traits.

### 1.13 Grandmont-Rosedale

- **Location**: Northwest Detroit, roughly bounded by Grand River Ave, Fenkell, Southfield Freeway, and Outer Drive
- **Character/Identity**: Five stable, well-organized neighborhoods (Rosedale Park, North Rosedale Park, Minock Park, Grandmont No. 1, Grandmont) that have resisted Detroit's decline through strong community action.
- **Current State**: Rosedale Park is among the largest historic districts in Michigan, with more than 1,600 architecturally distinct homes. GRDC has renovated and sold more than 100 formerly vacant and blighted houses. [Source: Grandmont Rosedale Development Corporation, https://www.grandmontrosedale.com/rosedale-park]
- **Key Landmarks**: Rosedale Park homes (founded 1916), annual home tours
- **Relevant History**: Homes built early-to-mid 20th century with steeply pitched gabled roofs, ornate wrought-iron doors, and brick masonry. Strong community identity maintained despite citywide decline.
- **Community Organizations**: Grandmont Rosedale Development Corporation (GRDC, founded 1989), Rosedale Park Improvement Association (RPIA, serving 1,600 homes), Grandmont Community Association. [Source: Model D, "After 25 years, Grandmont Rosedale provides a template for community development," https://modeldmedia.com/grandmont-rosedale-25th-072214/]
- **Game Transformation**: Model for community-led stabilization. Projects could support their existing approach: renovation, blight removal, community organizing. Template for "how to hold a neighborhood together."

### 1.14 Warrendale

- **Location**: Southwest border of Detroit
- **Character/Identity**: Once a safe middle-class community nicknamed "copper canyon" because it was heavily populated by police officers required to live in the city. Now a diverse neighborhood with growing Arab American population.
- **Current State**: Property values rising but crime persists. Significant Iraqi, Syrian refugee resettlement. [Source: Detroit News, "Property values rise in Detroit's Warrendale," https://www.detroitnews.com/story/news/local/detroit-city/2019/05/21/property-values-up-detroits-warrendale-neighborhood-but-crime-issues-persist/1164986001/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Warrendale Community Center
- **Relevant History**: Most houses built in the 1940s. When Michigan's legislature banned residency requirements for municipal employees in 1999, hundreds of police officers and families left for suburbs -- and "when the cops moved out, criminals and blight moved in." [Source: Wikipedia, "Warrendale, Detroit," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warrendale,_Detroit]
- **Community Organizations**: Warrendale Community Organization, Arab American community groups
- **Game Transformation**: Immigrant integration, community policing alternatives, public safety through community investment rather than enforcement.

### 1.15 Banglatown (Campau/Banglatown)

- **Location**: Straddling the Detroit-Hamtramck border along Conant Street (a.k.a. "Bangladesh Avenue")
- **Character/Identity**: A dynamic Bangladeshi American community hub, one of the largest concentrations of Bangladeshi Americans in the US.
- **Current State**: Over half of the ~5,000 residents identified as Asian (mostly Bangladeshi) in the 2010 Census, 30% African American. Conant Street hosts multiple Bangladeshi grocers, restaurants, and businesses. [Source: Model D, "Everyone knows about Banglatown," https://www.modeldmedia.com/features/Banglatown-Street-View-22.aspx]
- **Key Landmarks**: "Bangladesh Avenue" honorary street sign (vandalized in 2022), Zaf Super Market, various mosques and community centers
- **Relevant History**: Bangladeshi migration to Detroit area began in the 1930s; major growth since the 1990s. In 2008, the business district received the honorary title "Bangladesh Avenue." [Source: One New Humanity CDC, "Banglatown & Hamtramck," https://www.onenewhumanitycdc.org/blog/banglatown-hamtramck]
- **Community Organizations**: Bangladeshi American Public Affairs Committee, One New Humanity CDC
- **Game Transformation**: Cultural district development, immigrant entrepreneurship support, cross-cultural community building, international food systems.

### 1.16 North End

- **Location**: Just north of Midtown, historically the northern extension of Paradise Valley
- **Character/Identity**: Once a thriving Black entrepreneurial district; now home to Detroit's first urban agrihood (MUFI). A contested space between community agriculture and gentrification.
- **Current State**: Site of the Michigan Urban Farming Initiative's 3-acre agrihood. Property values have increased 20% in the past year due to gentrification pressure. [Source: Yes Magazine, "In Detroit, A New Type of Agricultural Neighborhood Has Emerged," https://www.yesmagazine.org/social-justice/2019/11/05/food-community-detroit-garden-agriculture]
- **Key Landmarks**: MUFI campus (America's First Sustainable Urban Agrihood), Oakland Avenue Urban Farm
- **Relevant History**: Originally part of Hamtramck Township, annexed 1891. I-75 was built in 1959, severing the North End from the city center and destroying Paradise Valley and Black Bottom. The neighborhood was the northern extension of Hastings Street, where Black entrepreneurship prospered. [Source: Wikipedia, "North End, Detroit," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_End,_Detroit]
- **Community Organizations**: Michigan Urban Farming Initiative (MUFI, founded 2011), North End Woodward Community Coalition (establishing a community solar trust), Oakland Avenue Urban Farm. MUFI has produced over 50,000 pounds of produce distributed to 2,000+ households within a 2-square-mile radius. [Source: MUFI, https://www.miufi.org/america-s-first-urban-agrihood]
- **Game Transformation**: Agrihood model. Tension between urban farming movement and gentrification. Community land trust to protect agricultural land. The North End community solar trust ($200,000 DOE grant) is a real solarpunk project.

### 1.17 West Village

- **Location**: Lower east side, near the Detroit River and Belle Isle
- **Character/Identity**: Walkable, trendy neighborhood with a strong local food and retail scene. Detroit's "Brooklyn" -- young professionals, craft businesses, historic homes.
- **Current State**: Growing restaurant and retail scene centered on Van Dyke Street. Known for Sister Pie (nationally recognized bakery), Marrow (James Beard-nominated butcher/restaurant), Red Hook Coffee. [Source: Visit Detroit, "The Perfect West Village How-To Guide," https://visitdetroit.com/inside-the-d/west-village-detroit/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Sister Pie, Marrow, Belle Isle Pizza, Darkroom Detroit
- **Relevant History**: Mix of early 20th-century homes including Arts and Crafts bungalows, Tudor revivals, and classic apartment buildings. Neighborhood association formed in the 1970s.
- **Community Organizations**: Historic West Village Association
- **Game Transformation**: Small business incubation, local food economy, walkable neighborhood model. Gateway to Belle Isle ecological restoration.

### 1.18 Brush Park

- **Location**: Between Downtown and Midtown, bounded by Woodward, Beaubien, Mack, and Fisher Freeway
- **Character/Identity**: Once nicknamed "Little Paris" for its grand Victorian mansions built for Detroit's elite in the 1850s-1890s. Experienced extreme blight before recent revival.
- **Current State**: Major redevelopment underway. Of the original ~300 homes (including 70 Victorian mansions), about 80 structures remain. New condominiums and the "City Modern" development (450 new homes) blend modern and historic architecture. [Source: Wikipedia, "Brush Park," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brush_Park]
- **Key Landmarks**: Ransom Gillis House (restored), remaining Victorian mansions, City Modern development
- **Relevant History**: Developed by Edmund Askin Brush starting in the 1850s. Severe restrictions required high-end mansion construction. By the early 20th century, affluent residents left; homes were divided into rooming houses for auto workers. Severe blight by the 1970s-80s. [Source: Detroit News, "The idyllic neighborhood of Ransom Gillis," https://www.detroitnews.com/story/news/local/michigan-history/2015/11/28/brush-park-history-ransom-gillis/76472086/]
- **Community Organizations**: Brush Park Community Development Corporation
- **Game Transformation**: Historic preservation + new development. Equity questions about who benefits from neighborhood revival. Mixed-income housing models.

### 1.19 New Center

- **Location**: 3 miles north of Downtown along Woodward Avenue, centered on the intersection of Grand Boulevard and Second Avenue
- **Character/Identity**: Detroit's original "second downtown," built in the 1920s as a business hub by the Fisher Brothers and General Motors. Art Deco architecture at its finest.
- **Current State**: Mixed commercial and residential. Fisher Building being revitalized. Henry Ford Health System is a major anchor. [Source: Wikipedia, "New Center, Detroit," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Center,_Detroit]
- **Key Landmarks**: Fisher Building (1928, Albert Kahn, ornate marbles, mosaics, painted ceilings -- dubbed "Detroit's largest art object"), Cadillac Place (former GM HQ, completed 1922, now state offices), Henry Ford Hospital [Source: Historic Detroit, "Fisher Building," https://historicdetroit.org/buildings/fisher-building]
- **Relevant History**: Developed as a business district north of Downtown beginning ~1915. GM and Fisher Body settled here in the 1920s due to limited space Downtown. GM headquarters from 1923-2001.
- **Community Organizations**: New Center Council
- **Game Transformation**: Adaptive reuse of Art Deco commercial buildings. Maker spaces, community institutions, transit hub (along Woodward corridor).

### 1.20 Livernois-McNichols (Avenue of Fashion)

- **Location**: Northwest Detroit, centered on the intersection of Livernois Avenue and McNichols Road (Six Mile)
- **Character/Identity**: Historic Black-owned commercial corridor once known as the "Avenue of Fashion" for its high-end garments and specialty goods in the 1950s-60s.
- **Current State**: $17 million Livernois Streetscape Project completed: 24-foot-wide sidewalks, protected bike lanes, new landscaping, double-crosswalk streetlights. 18 African American-led businesses opened since completion. In 2026, Michigan awarded a $4.3 million grant for corridor extension. [Source: Detroit News, "Detroit turns a commercial corridor into a new kind of hot spot," https://www.detroitnews.com/story/news/local/detroit-city/2022/08/08/detroit-hotspot-isnt-new-its-revived-livernois-avenue-of-fashion-streetscapes-strategic-neighborhood/10060181002/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Ella Fitzgerald Park and Greenway, University of Detroit Mercy
- **Relevant History**: Through the 1960s, one of the city's busiest shopping districts. Declined with the rise of suburban shopping malls. [Source: LookUp Detroit, "The Storied History of Detroit's Livernois-McNichols," https://lookupdetroit.com/the-storied-history-of-detroits-livernois-mcnichols-neighborhood-w-video/]
- **Community Organizations**: Live6 Alliance (formed 2015), ProsperUs
- **Game Transformation**: Commercial corridor revitalization, Black entrepreneurship, complete streets design, community wealth building. Real success story to model in-game.

### 1.21 Rouge Park Area

- **Location**: West side, along the Rouge River
- **Character/Identity**: Michigan's largest urban park (1,184 acres -- larger than Central Park at 843 acres and Belle Isle at 982 acres). A vast natural area with old-growth forest, prairie, and working farms.
- **Current State**: Celebrated its 100th anniversary in 2025. Contains 4.6 miles of Rouge River, 400+ acres of forest, 60 acres of native prairie, and a 5-acre marsh. Features D-Town Farm (Detroit Black Community Food Security Network) and a Buffalo Soldiers living museum. [Source: Click On Detroit, "As Rouge Park celebrates 100 years," https://www.clickondetroit.com/sponsored/2025/06/11/as-rouge-park-celebrates-100-years-the-city-and-local-partners-prioritize-the-future-for-detroit-parks/]
- **Key Landmarks**: D-Town Farm (7-acre urban farm growing 36+ fruits, vegetables, herbs), one of the largest old-growth forests in SE Michigan, Scout Hollow Campground, Brennan Pool
- **Relevant History**: City acquired the land from several farmers for $1.3 million in the 1920s; opened 1925. Declined in 1960s-70s as population dropped. Friends of Rouge Park formed in 2002 to prevent its abandonment. [Source: Friends of Rouge Park, https://www.rougepark.org/rouge-park-history]
- **Community Organizations**: Friends of Rouge Park, Detroit Black Community Food Security Network (D-Town Farm)
- **Game Transformation**: Major ecological restoration site. Urban forest management, prairie restoration, food sovereignty, river cleanup. The Detroit Sugarbush Project operates here, connecting indigenous land stewardship to urban ecology. [Source: PBS News, "Activists tap a sweet Indigenous tradition," https://www.pbs.org/newshour/education/activists-tap-a-sweet-indigenous-tradition-to-connect-youth-of-color-in-detroit-with-the-outdoors]

### 1.22 Gratiot/7 Mile

- **Location**: Northeast Detroit, around the intersection of Gratiot Avenue and 7 Mile Road
- **Character/Identity**: One of Detroit's hardest-hit areas with the highest vacancy among the city's 10 Strategic Neighborhood Fund (SNF) target areas.
- **Current State**: ~1,500 abandoned properties at the start of the SNF planning process. Neighborhood stabilization is the top priority. [Source: City of Detroit, "Gratiot/7 Mile improvements," https://detroitmi.gov/news/city-detroit-begins-implementing-gratiot7-mile-improvements-part-strategic-neighborhood-fund]
- **Key Landmarks**: Gratiot Avenue commercial corridor
- **Relevant History**: Like much of Detroit's east side, affected by deindustrialization, white flight, and decades of disinvestment.
- **Community Organizations**: Gratiot 7 Mile Community, various faith institutions
- **Game Transformation**: Massive vacancy = blank canvas. Demolition and greening strategy, community agriculture, wildlife corridors, "smart decline" approaches.

### 1.23 Osborn

- **Location**: Northeast Detroit, bounded by 8 Mile, McNichols, Gratiot, and Van Dyke
- **Character/Identity**: A deeply challenged east side neighborhood that has experienced dramatic population loss and blight.
- **Current State**: Population declined 27.3% between 2000-2010; children/youth decreased by 5,912 (39.3% decline). Described as "a cracked, empty shell" with broken windows, overgrown weeds, and abandoned homes. [Source: Wikipedia, "Osborn, Detroit," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osborn,_Detroit]
- **Key Landmarks**: Osborn Neighborhood Alliance facilities
- **Community Organizations**: Osborn Neighborhood Alliance
- **Game Transformation**: Community rebuilding from near-zero. Youth programs, blight removal, community safety, green infrastructure.

### 1.24 Morningside

- **Location**: East side of Detroit
- **Character/Identity**: A neighborhood showing that community organizing can reverse decline. Originally a French settlement of ribbon farms in the late 1700s; blossomed in the 1920s.
- **Current State**: After 27% population loss (2000-2010), a decade-long transformation driven by nonprofits has turned vacant lots into parks, cleaned dumping grounds, and improved owner-occupancy rates. [Source: Morningside Community Organization, https://www.morningsidedetroit.org/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Brick Tudor houses with steep roofs, symmetrical Colonials, mature trees from the 1920s
- **Relevant History**: Began as French ribbon farms in the 1700s. Middle-class residential boom in the 1920s.
- **Community Organizations**: Morningside Community Organization (active, effective)
- **Game Transformation**: Community-led recovery model. Vacant lot conversion, neighbor organizing, property stabilization. A "before and after" demonstration tile.

### 1.25 Jefferson Chalmers

- **Location**: Lower east side, along the Detroit River
- **Character/Identity**: A waterfront neighborhood facing the front lines of climate change in Detroit. Historic canal neighborhood with unique flooding challenges.
- **Current State**: Severe flooding in 2019-2020 destroyed basements and property. In 2021, FEMA designated it a flood hazard zone, requiring expensive flood insurance and restricting development. Tiger dams (emergency flood barriers) cost Detroit taxpayers $1.4 million. [Source: Planet Detroit, "Detroit's Jefferson Chalmers neighborhood looks to seawalls," https://planetdetroit.org/2025/10/jefferson-chalmers-flood-solution/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Residential canals, riverfront access, Mariner Park
- **Relevant History**: Record-high Great Lakes water levels combined with aging combined sewer infrastructure create a dual flooding threat -- from the river and from backed-up sewers. [Source: Detroit PBS, "Jefferson Chalmers neighborhood faces uncertain future," https://www.detroitpbs.org/news-media/one-detroit/jefferson-chalmers-neighborhood-faces-uncertain-future-due-to-climate-change/]
- **Community Organizations**: Jefferson Chalmers Community Council
- **Game Transformation**: Climate adaptation showcase tile. Green infrastructure, seawalls, wetland buffers, sewer separation, managed retreat discussions. The most climate-vulnerable neighborhood in the game.

### 1.26 Islandview

- **Location**: Lower east side, between Jefferson and Mack Avenues
- **Character/Identity**: Named for its views of Belle Isle across the Detroit River. A mix of historic charm and emerging development, targeted by the Strategic Neighborhood Fund.
- **Current State**: SNF planning process (published July 2020) aims for economic growth, affordable housing, and development with resident input. Older residents in crumbling homes face displacement fears. [Source: Bridge Michigan, "Islandview: New development, fears of displacement," https://www.bridgemi.com/urban-affairs/islandview-new-development-fears-displacement]
- **Key Landmarks**: Views of Belle Isle, historic French colonial-era street layouts (from ribbon farm era)
- **Relevant History**: Originated from French colonial ribbon farms in the 1700s. Influenced by the City Beautiful Movement in the late 19th century (Grand Boulevard, 1891-1913). [Source: The Neighborhoods, "Islandview," http://www.theneighborhoods.org/neighborhoods/islandview]
- **Community Organizations**: Islandview Village Development Corporation
- **Game Transformation**: Equitable development, anti-displacement, community land trusts. Bridge between Belle Isle restoration and east side neighborhoods.

### 1.27 Woodbridge

- **Location**: West of Midtown, adjacent to Wayne State University
- **Character/Identity**: A proud, culturally diverse neighborhood known for Victorian homes, front-porch culture, and a strong arts community. Long a favorite for musicians, performers, and artists.
- **Current State**: National Register of Historic Places (1980). Recognized as an intact, turn-of-the-century streetcar suburb. [Source: Woodbridge Neighborhood Development Corporation, https://www.woodbridgendc.org/neighborhood-history]
- **Key Landmarks**: Victorian-era homes, Trumbull Avenue corridor, Woodbridge Pub
- **Relevant History**: Named after Governor William Woodbridge (1840-41). Most buildings built after 1870. During WWII, homes were divided into apartments for defense workers. Wayne State and the city cleared 254 acres (half of Woodbridge) during 1960s urban renewal. Residents organized a Citizens' District Council to save the rest. [Source: Wikipedia, "Woodbridge, Detroit," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodbridge,_Detroit]
- **Community Organizations**: Woodbridge Neighborhood Development Corporation, Woodbridge Citizens' District Council
- **Game Transformation**: Historic preservation, artist co-ops, community resistance to institutional expansion. Model of successful community organizing against urban renewal.

### 1.28 Bagley

- **Location**: Northwest Detroit, near the Livernois-McNichols area
- **Character/Identity**: A historically Black middle- and upper-middle-class neighborhood with beautiful 1920s-30s homes and deep connections to Motown history.
- **Current State**: Active 90+ year-old community council. Vibrant business district along borders with Caribbean, Hibachi restaurants, grocery stores, art galleries, fashion boutiques. [Source: Bagley Community Council, https://bagleycommunity.org/about/history/]
- **Key Landmarks**: Mid-century to Tudor, Colonial, Gothic Revival homes. Music history: Stevie Wonder, Marvin Gaye, Berry Gordy gathered at Bagley kitchen tables to write songs.
- **Relevant History**: Building lots sold in the 1920s-30s by Detroit Land Contract Company with deed restrictions. African American families moved in during the 1960s; white flight accelerated after the 1967 uprising and court-ordered school busing.
- **Community Organizations**: Bagley Community Council (active 90+ years)
- **Game Transformation**: Black cultural preservation, Motown heritage, community wealth preservation, commercial corridor support.

### 1.29 Core City

- **Location**: Just northwest of Corktown and Woodbridge, centered on Grand River and Warren intersection
- **Character/Identity**: An emerging arts neighborhood with an eclectic mix of historic and modern architecture, including innovative micro-housing experiments.
- **Current State**: Experiencing revitalization with new single-family homes and townhouses being built alongside creative community spaces. [Source: Detroitisit, "Core City: What Is It? Why Is It?," https://detroitisit.com/core-city-what-is-it-why-is-it-where-is-it-whos-in-it/]
- **Key Landmarks**: True North Detroit (Phil Kafka's Quonset steel hut community, completed 2017), Humboldt Forest (graffiti art forum), annual Core City Night Market
- **Relevant History**: Shared Detroit's struggles with deindustrialization and urban decay. Core City Neighborhoods Inc. built ~100 single-family houses and townhomes in the early 2000s for moderate-to-low-income residents.
- **Community Organizations**: Core City Neighborhoods Inc.
- **Game Transformation**: Alternative housing models, maker spaces, arts-based revitalization. True North Detroit's Quonset huts are a real-world solarpunk-adjacent project.

## 2. Detroit Historical Events (for the Event System)

### 2.1 Detroit Bankruptcy and Emergency Management (2013-2014)

- **What Happened**: On July 18, 2013, Detroit filed for Chapter 9 bankruptcy -- the largest municipal bankruptcy in US history, with $18-20 billion in estimated debt. Michigan Governor Rick Snyder had appointed Kevyn Orr as "emergency manager" in March 2013, stripping elected officials of governing power. The bankruptcy was approved by the court in November 2014, restructuring or eliminating $7 billion in debt. Detroit exited bankruptcy in December 2014. [Source: Wikipedia, "Detroit bankruptcy," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detroit_bankruptcy]
- **Human Cost**: City workers (including police and firefighters) had pay cut. Employees were forced to take unpaid days off. Retirees lost portions of their pensions and all retiree health coverage. [Source: Axios Detroit, "Revisiting Detroit's bankruptcy," https://www.axios.com/local/detroit/2023/07/18/bankruptcy-revisited-detroit-michigan-2013]
- **Aftermath**: Income tax revenues climbed from $254 million (2014) to $402 million. Over 24,000 vacant houses demolished. [Source: Detroit Regional Chamber, "Detroit is Reborn 10 Years After," https://www.detroitchamber.com/detroit-is-reborn-10-years-after-the-largest-municipal-bankruptcy-in-the-united-states/]
- **Game Event**: "Fiscal Emergency" -- player must navigate between austerity measures that harm residents and restructuring that enables recovery. Tension between state-imposed emergency management and local democratic control.

### 2.2 Water Shutoff Crisis (2014-ongoing)

- **What Happened**: In 2014, the Detroit Water and Sewerage Department (DWSD) began the largest residential water shutoff in US history, terminating service to over 20,000 homes. In 2015 alone, 23,883 households (~64,000 people) lost water service, despite 38% of the population living below the poverty line. [Source: NAACP LDF, "Responding for Detroit Water Shutoff Crisis," https://www.naacpldf.org/case-issue/detroit-water-shutoff-crisis/]
- **Human Rights Response**: UN officials visited Detroit in October 2014 and declared the shutoffs a "violation of the human right to water." The ACLU and NAACP LDF filed class action lawsuits (Lyda et al. v. City of Detroit). Dramatic racial disparities: shutoffs far more likely in majority-Black neighborhoods. [Source: ACLU of Michigan, "Water Shutoffs in Detroit," https://www.aclumich.org/en/cases/water-shutoffs-detroit]
- **Community Response**: Organizations including Michigan Welfare Rights Organization, People's Water Board, and We the People of Detroit organized water deliveries, fought for a moratorium during COVID, and advocated for income-based water rates. [Source: Planet Detroit, "A moral crisis: Metro Detroit faith leaders call for end to water shutoffs," https://planetdetroit.org/2022/03/a-moral-crisis-metro-detroit-faith-leaders-call-for-end-to-water-shutoffs-adoption-of-income-based-rates/]
- **Game Event**: "Water Shutoff Crisis" -- choice between paying utility debts (depletes budget) or fighting for water as a human right (gains community trust but creates political opposition). Recurring event until water affordability policy is enacted.

### 2.3 Auto Industry Collapse and Deindustrialization

- **What Happened**: Between 1947 and 1963, GM, Ford, and Chrysler built 25 new plants -- all in the suburbs, not Detroit. Over 143,000 jobs lost. Detroit's economy, once entirely dependent on auto manufacturing, collapsed as factories moved to suburbs, the Sun Belt, and overseas. [Source: Wikipedia, "Decline of Detroit," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decline_of_Detroit]
- **Scale**: The vast majority of Detroit's population loss was directly tied to deindustrialization moving factories from the inner city to suburbs. [Source: Dollars & Sense, "Detroit and Deindustrialization," https://www.dollarsandsense.org/detroit-and-deindustrialization/]
- **Game Event**: "Legacy of the Assembly Line" -- an event exploring the tension between industrial heritage and post-industrial futures. Could involve proposals for factory site reuse, manufacturing co-ops, or green industry.

### 2.4 1967 Rebellion/Uprising

- **What Happened**: Beginning July 23, 1967, after a police raid on an unlicensed after-hours bar on the Near West Side. Lasted five days. 43 deaths, hundreds of injuries, ~1,700 fires, 7,000+ arrests. One of the deadliest social insurgences in American history. [Source: Detroit Historical Society, "Uprising of 1967," https://www.detroithistorical.org/learn/online-research/encyclopedia-of-detroit/uprising-1967]
- **Underlying Causes**: Decades of institutional racism and entrenched segregation: economic hardship from deindustrialization (Black workers "felt the brunt"), housing discrimination (Black residents paying higher rents, only 39% homeownership vs. 64% for whites in 1960), and systemic police brutality. [Source: Britannica, "Detroit Riot of 1967," https://www.britannica.com/event/Detroit-Riot-of-1967]
- **Legacy**: Accelerated white flight; many destroyed buildings never rebuilt. But also ushered in a new era of Black activism: organizations like New Detroit and Focus: HOPE were founded. Contributed to election of Detroit's first Black mayor (Coleman Young, 1974). [Source: PBS, "How the 1967 riots reshaped Detroit," https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/1967-riots-reshaped-detroit-rebuilding-still-needs-done]
- **Game Event**: "Anniversary of the Uprising" -- a yearly event that triggers community reflection, demands for police reform, and potential for either healing or renewed tension.

### 2.5 White Flight and Population Decline

- **What Happened**: Detroit peaked at 1.85 million people in 1950. By 2020, approximately 640,000 remained -- a 65% loss. Approximately 1.4 million of 1.6 million white residents after WWII left for the suburbs. [Source: Wikipedia, "Decline of Detroit," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decline_of_Detroit]
- **Causes**: Great Migration brought hundreds of thousands of Black people from the South seeking employment. White flight was driven by racial prejudice, suburbanization, highway construction, and deindustrialization.
- **Game Mechanic**: The starting condition of the game. Every vacant lot, every abandoned building, every food desert is a consequence of this history. Population change should be a visible metric.

### 2.6 Urban Farming Movement

- **D-Town Farm**: Operated by the Detroit Black Community Food Security Network. 7-acre farm on the west side (in Rouge Park) growing 36+ fruits, vegetables, and herbs. Executive Director Malik Yakini emphasizes "fostering self-determination." [Source: Visit Detroit, "Detroit's Greener Side," https://visitdetroit.com/inside-the-d/urban-farming-detroit/]
- **Michigan Urban Farming Initiative (MUFI)**: 3-acre farm in the North End, America's first sustainable urban agrihood. 10,000+ volunteers, 100,000+ hours, 50,000+ pounds of produce distributed to 2,000+ households. [Source: MUFI, https://www.miufi.org/america-s-first-urban-agrihood]
- **Keep Growing Detroit**: Founded 2013, helped establish 1,500+ gardens and farms citywide. Mission: promote food sovereignty. [Source: American Association of Geographers, "Sowing the Seeds for Food Sovereignty in Detroit," https://www.aag.org/sowing-the-seeds-for-food-sovereignty-in-detroit/]
- **Game Event**: "Urban Farm Proposal" -- community group requests land for an urban farm. Player chooses location, scale, and whether to support food sovereignty or market-based agriculture.

### 2.7 The Heidelberg Project

- **What Happened**: In 1986, artist Tyree Guyton returned to Heidelberg Street on Detroit's East Side, where he grew up, and found it devastated by drugs and poverty. Encouraged by his grandfather, he began transforming vacant lots and abandoned houses into massive art installations using found objects and refuse. [Source: The Heidelberg Project, "History," https://www.heidelberg.org/history]
- **Impact**: Now internationally recognized, the Heidelberg Project draws 275,000+ tourists annually and generates ~$3.2 million in income for the Detroit community. Employs longtime community members. [Source: Kresge Foundation, "Tyree Guyton tapped as 2026 Kresge Eminent Artist," https://kresge.org/news-views/pathbreaking-painter-arts-activist-and-heidelberg-project-founder-tyree-guyton-tapped-as-2026-kresge-eminent-artist/]
- **Game Event**: "Community Art Project" -- an artist proposes transforming vacant lots into art. Boosts community trust and tourism but may face opposition from those wanting traditional development.

### 2.8 Land Bank and Vacant Property Challenges

- **Scale**: An estimated 19-24 square miles of vacant land scattered throughout Detroit. The Detroit Land Bank Authority (DLBA) is the largest land bank in the US, owning/managing ~100,000 parcels (~66,000 directly controlled) of vacant lots and abandoned houses forced into tax foreclosure. [Source: University of Michigan, "Meet the Future: Transforming Vacant Land," https://seas.umich.edu/stewards/spring-2023/meet-future-transforming-vacant-land-detroit]
- **Programs**: Side Lot Program allows neighbors to purchase adjacent vacant lots for $100. DLBA launched a $15 million Affordable Housing Pipeline. [Source: DLBA, https://buildingdetroit.org/]
- **Game Mechanic**: The tile map itself. Every "vacant" tile represents this reality. The DLBA model should inform how players acquire and transform land.

### 2.9 Marathon Oil Refinery and Delray Environmental Justice

- **Details**: See Delray neighborhood section (1.9) above.
- **Game Event**: "Refinery Expansion Proposal" or "Environmental Violation Report" -- player must choose between economic benefits and environmental/health consequences for the community.

### 2.10 Great Lakes Water Politics

- **Scale**: The Great Lakes contain one-fifth of the world's fresh surface water, serving 28+ million people. [Source: International Joint Commission, "Great Lakes Water Quality," https://www.ijc.org/en/what/glwq]
- **Current Tensions**: US-Canada joint management of four of five Great Lakes now at risk due to political tensions and trade disputes. Data center boom exposes critical gaps in Michigan water policy. [Source: Planet Detroit, "US-Canada Great Lakes water-sharing at risk," https://planetdetroit.org/2025/03/us-canada-great-lakes-water-sharing/]
- **Game Event**: "Great Lakes Water Compact Challenge" -- external entity seeks to divert Great Lakes water. Player must build regional coalitions to protect water sovereignty. Endgame/Beyond the Map stage event.

### 2.11 Belle Isle Becoming a State Park (2013)

- **What Happened**: Michigan signed a 30-year lease to manage Belle Isle as a state park, saving Detroit millions in annual maintenance costs but transferring control from the city to the state. [Source: Belle Isle Conservancy, https://www.belleisleconservancy.org/about-belle-isle]
- **Game Event**: "State Takeover Proposal" -- state government offers to take over management of a city asset. Saves budget but reduces local control and may impose access fees.

### 2.12 Gilbert/Bedrock Downtown Development

- **What Happened**: Dan Gilbert's Bedrock has invested billions in Downtown Detroit real estate, generating economic activity but raising serious concerns about gentrification, affordability, and corporate influence. A 2017 ad showing a predominantly white crowd with the slogan "See Detroit Like We Do" generated enormous backlash as "psychological gentrification." Gilbert was allowed to skirt affordable housing mandates for new towers. [Source: ProPublica, "How a Tax Break to Help the Poor Went to NBA Owner Dan Gilbert," https://www.propublica.org/article/how-a-tax-break-to-help-the-poor-went-to-nba-owner-dan-gilbert]
- **Game Event**: "Billionaire Developer Proposal" -- a wealthy developer offers massive investment but on terms that benefit corporations over communities. Player must negotiate community benefits agreements, affordable housing requirements, and democratic oversight.

### 2.13 Community Land Trusts

- **What They Are**: CLTs own land permanently, ensuring long-term affordability. In Detroit, organizations including Detroit Cultivator (North End), Dream of Detroit (Dexter-Linwood), Common Grounds CLT (Islandview), and Avalon Village (Highland Park) are establishing CLTs. [Source: Outlier Media, "5 nonprofits look to start community land trusts in Detroit," https://outliermedia.org/detroit-community-land-trusts/]
- **Game Mechanic**: Community Land Trust should be a project type that permanently protects tiles from gentrification and displacement.

### 2.14 Detroit Future City Framework Plan

- **What It Is**: A 50-year land use planning document released in 2012, recommending transformation of Detroit's land use, including converting large expanses of vacant land to green infrastructure, urban agriculture, and managed natural areas. [Source: Detroit Future City, https://detroitfuturecity.com/our-programs/]
- **Key Concept**: "Detroit GreenSpace" -- citywide effort to transform 18 square miles of vacant land into protected natural areas.
- **Game Mechanic**: The overall framework for the game's land transformation system. This real plan provides the intellectual foundation for the game's core mechanic of converting vacant land to productive ecological uses.

### 2.15 Highland Park Streetlight Removal

- **What Happened**: In 2011, DTE Energy repossessed 1,400 streetlight poles when Highland Park couldn't pay its $4 million utility debt ($60,000/month electric bill). Workers literally pulled poles from the ground without informing residents. [Source: Next City, "The Utility Repossessed Their City's Streetlights," https://nextcity.org/urbanist-news/utility-repossessed-citys-streetlights-now-theyre-relighting-the-night]
- **Community Response**: Soulardarity formed to install solar streetlights as community-owned infrastructure. [Source: Model D, "Illuminating Highland Park, one solar streetlight at a time," https://modeldmedia.com/soulardarity-highland-park-082514/]
- **Game Event**: "Utility Shutoff Threat" -- utility company threatens to cut services to a neighborhood over unpaid bills. Player can pay the debt (budget), negotiate, or invest in community-owned alternatives (solar).

## 3. Ecological Context

### 3.1 Great Lakes Ecosystem and Freshwater Significance

- The Great Lakes contain one-fifth of the world's fresh surface water supply and are the largest freshwater ecosystem on Earth, supporting rare and globally unique species. [Source: International Joint Commission, "Great Lakes Water Quality," https://www.ijc.org/en/what/glwq]
- They provide drinking water to approximately 28-30 million people and support manufacturing, transportation, farming, tourism, recreation, and energy production. [Source: Great Lakes Literacy, MSU Extension, https://www.canr.msu.edu/news/great_lakes_literacy_principle_one_the_great_lakes_bodies_of_fresh_water_wi]
- Freshwater is the defining ecological resource of the game. Detroit sits at the heart of the largest freshwater system on Earth -- this should be the core environmental asset and the key to the "Beyond the Map" endgame.

### 3.2 Detroit River Ecology and Contamination History

- **Historical Contamination**: In the 1960s, the Detroit River was one of the most polluted rivers in North America. In 1970, the entire fishing industry was closed due to toxic mercury levels. Negative impacts included genetic mutations in bacteria, death of macroinvertebrates, contaminant accumulation in insects, clams, fish, and ducks, and tumor formation in fish. [Source: EPA, "Detroit River AOC," https://www.epa.gov/great-lakes-aocs/detroit-river-aoc]
- **Contaminated Sediments**: Up to 4 million cubic yards of contaminated sediments (PCBs, PAHs, and other contaminants) rest at the bottom of the Detroit River. EPA estimates $1 billion needed for complete cleanup. [Source: Great Lakes Now, "Detroit River Restoration," https://www.greatlakesnow.org/2019/10/detroit-river-restoration-progress-remediation-epa/]
- **Restoration**: Named an "Area of Concern" (AOC) under the 1987 Great Lakes Water Quality Agreement. EPA's Great Lakes Restoration Initiative (2010) funded 14 habitat restoration projects, 13 of 14 completed as of 2025, totaling $50 million. Species returning: bald eagles, ospreys, peregrine falcons, lake whitefish, sturgeon, walleye, salmon. [Source: NOAA Fisheries, "Revitalizing the Detroit River Ecosystem," https://www.fisheries.noaa.gov/feature-story/revitalizing-detroit-river-ecosystem-great-lakes-fisheries]
- **Game Mechanic**: River tiles should start contaminated and progressively clean up. Aquatic species return as ecological health improves.

### 3.3 Rouge River Restoration

- **Watershed**: 466 square miles draining into the Detroit River across 48 communities. Contains the oldest and most heavily populated/industrialized area in southeast Michigan. [Source: Friends of the Rouge, https://therouge.org/rouge-river/]
- **Contamination**: Sediment and water contamination from industrial development, combined/sanitary sewer overflows, nonpoint source pollution.
- **Progress**: Once one of the most degraded urban streams in the country, now a model for river restoration. Dissolved oxygen levels improved enough for fish and macroinvertebrates to return. Now clean enough for a 27-mile water trail for kayaking/canoeing. Nine habitat restoration projects ($12.7 million) completed or near completion. [Source: Detroit News, "With cleanup efforts, Rouge River 'has come a long way'," https://www.detroitnews.com/story/news/local/wayne-county/2018/05/19/rouge-river-cleanup-efforts/35099307/]
- **Key Organization**: Friends of the Rouge (founded 1986), Alliance of Rouge Communities. [Source: EPA, "Rouge River AOC," https://www.epa.gov/great-lakes-aocs/rouge-river-aoc]
- **Game Mechanic**: Multi-turn, multi-tile restoration project. Progressive cleanup unlocks recreation, fishing, and wildlife corridor benefits.

### 3.4 Urban Heat Island Data for Detroit

- **Current**: In a typical year around 1990, Detroit experienced ~7 days above 91.3 degrees F per year.
- **Projected (2050)**: ~35 days per year over 91.3 degrees F -- nearly a full additional month of 90-degree days. [Source: ClimateCheck, "Detroit Climate Change Risks," https://climatecheck.com/michigan/detroit]
- **Great Lakes Region**: Average air temperatures projected to increase 3-6 degrees F (1.7-3.3 degrees C) by 2050. Lake surface temperatures may rise up to 7 degrees F by 2050. [Source: U.S. Climate Resilience Toolkit, "Great Lakes," https://toolkit.climate.gov/regions/great-lakes/building-resilience-great-lakes]
- **Precipitation**: Annual precipitation projected to increase from ~31.4" to ~33.4", with increases in intensity and duration of heaviest rainfalls. [Source: Bridge Michigan, "Detroit expects more extreme heat, flooding," https://bridgemi.com/michigan-environment-watch/detroit-expects-more-extreme-heat-flooding-amid-climate-change/]
- **Game Mechanic**: These projections should drive the climate pressure system. Urban heat island effect should be tile-specific, affected by tree canopy, green infrastructure, and impervious surface coverage.

### 3.5 Soil Contamination from Industrial Legacy

- **Lead**: Homes and soils across Detroit contaminated with lead from heavy industry and lead-based paint. Lead mainly comes from burning leaded gasoline (historical) and paint. Disproportionate impacts on children. [Source: Planet Detroit, "Getting the lead out of Detroit's soil," https://planetdetroit.org/2021/08/getting-the-lead-out-of-detroits-soil/]
- **Specific Areas**: Fly ash mainly concentrated in Southwest Detroit (heavy industry). Delray has extensive air and soil pollution from decades of industrial waste. Over 1,000 brownfield sites citywide as of 2016. [Source: Bridge Detroit, "EGLE awards $5.8M to redevelop contaminated sites," https://www.bridgedetroit.com/redevelop-contaminated-sites-detroit/]
- **Contaminant Types**: Polychlorinated naphthalenes (PCNs), polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) found in road dust and residential soils near industrial sites. Increased residential brownfield density within 200m associated with elevated serum lead and mercury levels. [Source: Michigan Public, "Study finds contaminants in Detroit soil and street dust," https://www.michiganpublic.org/environment-climate-change/2025-02-06/study-finds-contaminants-in-detroit-soil-and-street-dust]
- **Solar on Brownfields**: Michigan is exploring solar energy installations on contaminated brownfield land. [Source: Planet Detroit, "Can solar projects transform Michigan's contaminated land?," https://planetdetroit.org/2025/05/michigan-brownfields-solar-energy/]
- **Game Mechanic**: Tiles should have contamination levels that must be remediated before food projects can be placed. Different contamination types (lead, industrial chemicals, petroleum) require different remediation approaches and timelines.

### 3.6 Climate Projections for Great Lakes Region

- Temperature: +3-6 degrees F by 2050 [Source: GLISA, "Temperature," https://glisa.umich.edu/resources-tools/climate-impacts/temperature/]
- More intense precipitation events, increased flooding risk
- Loss of ice cover on Great Lakes (affecting lake-effect weather patterns, evaporation, ecosystems)
- Longer growing seasons (potentially beneficial for agriculture)
- Increased invasive species pressure
- "Michigan 2050: hot, wet, and no ice" [Source: Planet Detroit, "Michigan climate change," https://planetdetroit.org/2021/09/michigan-2050-hot-wet-and-no-ice/]
- **Game Mechanic**: Climate pressure should escalate with specific Great Lakes impacts: more flooding, more extreme heat, longer growing seasons (mixed blessing), ice loss affecting lake ecosystems.

### 3.7 Urban Wildlife in Detroit

- **Species**: Squirrels, raccoons, opossums, skunks, foxes, pheasants, coyotes, deer, wild turkeys, bald eagles, beavers. Over 250 bird species. Estimated 1,000+ coyotes with established territories throughout the city. [Source: WXYZ, "Wildlife is making a comeback in urban areas," https://www.wxyz.com/news/wildlife-is-making-a-comeback-in-urban-areas-of-metro-detroit-heres-why]
- **Cause**: As Detroit's population declined, ~one-third of the city transformed into vacant land, creating urban habitat. Tall grass serves as shelter for pheasants and other wildlife. Beavers have returned to Detroit waterways after nearly a century. [Source: Planet Detroit, "Detroit Zoo leads coalition to connect habitat restoration," https://planetdetroit.org/2023/01/detroit-zoo-leads-coalition-to-connect-habitat-restoration-efforts-across-the-city/]
- **Research**: Yale researchers actively studying how wildlife adapts to urban settings in Detroit. Detroit Zoo leading a coalition to connect habitat restoration efforts across the city. [Source: Detroit News, "Wildlife in Detroit: Yale research team," https://www.detroitnews.com/story/news/local/detroit-city/2023/11/03/yale-researchers-study-detroit-wild-animals-like-coyotes-and-foxes/71209524007/]
- **Game Mechanic**: Wildlife should appear on tiles as ecological health improves. Different species appear at different thresholds. Pheasants on vacant lots, deer in green corridors, eagles near restored waterways, beavers in river areas. This is a unique and characterful feature of Detroit's ecology.

### 3.8 Connection to Broader Ecology

- Detroit sits at the nexus of multiple ecological zones: Great Lakes aquatic ecosystem, Great Plains prairie remnants (found in Rouge Park), Eastern deciduous forest
- The Rouge Park old-growth forest is one of the largest in southeast Michigan
- 60 acres of native prairie in Rouge Park connect to broader grassland ecology
- The Detroit River is a critical migratory corridor for fish and birds

## 4. Political Context

### 4.1 Detroit City Council Structure

- **Composition**: 9 members -- 7 elected from single-member districts, 2 elected at-large. Current structure approved by voters in November 2009, effective 2013. [Source: Wikipedia, "Detroit City Council," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detroit_City_Council]
- **Powers**: Adoption of ordinances, approval of the municipal budget, confirmation of mayoral appointees, authorization for sale/disposition of city-owned property. [Source: City of Detroit, "City Council," https://detroitmi.gov/government/city-council]
- **Operations**: Full-time council required to meet every business day for at least 10 months of the year, with at least 8 meetings per year at locations besides City Hall. Officers: President and President Pro Tempore, elected from among members for 4-year terms.
- **Game Mechanic**: 9 council members with district affiliations. Player needs council votes for major policies and budget approval. Different districts represent different neighborhoods and constituencies.

### 4.2 Mayor's Powers and Constraints

- **Structure**: Detroit has a "strong mayoral" system. The mayor is chief executive, appoints department directors, proposes the budget, and serves as conservator of the peace. [Source: Wikipedia, "Government of Detroit," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Detroit]
- **Key Constraint**: Council approves budgets, but the mayor is not obligated to adhere to council earmarking. The mayor cannot unilaterally enact ordinances or dispose of city property.
- **Game Mechanic**: The player (mayor) proposes; the council disposes. This creates the political tension that the critic review identified as missing.

### 4.3 Relationship with Michigan State Government

- **Emergency Manager**: Michigan has extraordinary power to override local democracy through emergency manager appointments (see 4.4 below).
- **State Park Control**: Belle Isle transferred to state management (2013).
- **Funding Dependencies**: Detroit depends on state revenue sharing, which has been cut repeatedly over decades.
- **Game Mechanic**: State government as an external force that can help or hinder. State funding as a budget source with strings attached. Emergency manager as a game-over or major setback condition.

### 4.4 Emergency Manager History (PA 436)

- **Legal Framework**: Public Act 436 (effective March 28, 2013) allows Michigan's governor to appoint emergency managers who assume all authority of locally elected officials. PA 436 succeeded PA 4 (2011), which Michigan voters rejected by referendum in 2012 -- only to see PA 436 passed a month later with a provision that the public could not repeal it. [Source: Michigan Public, "How did we get here? A look back at Michigan's emergency manager law," https://www.michiganpublic.org/politics-government/2016-02-03/how-did-we-get-here-a-look-back-at-michigans-emergency-manager-law]
- **Impact on Democracy**: Emergency managers in Pontiac, Flint, Benton Harbor, River Rouge, Highland Park, and Detroit disproportionately impacted political rights of people of color. Under PA 436, the governor's appointee acts "for and in place of local governing bodies." [Source: ACLU of Michigan, "Emergency Manager Law," https://www.aclumich.org/cases/emergency-manager-law/]
- **Connection to Flint**: PA 436 decisions by emergency managers directly led to the Flint Water Crisis (2016). [Source: MSU Extension, "A Review of Michigan's Local Financial Emergency Law," https://www.canr.msu.edu/resources/a_review_of_michigans_local_financial_emergency_law]
- **Game Mechanic**: Major threat mechanic. If the player's budget goes into severe deficit, the state can impose an emergency manager, essentially ending democratic governance and the player's agency. This is the most Detroit-specific lose condition possible.

### 4.5 Community Organizing Traditions

- **We the People of Detroit (WPD)**: Co-founded 2008 in response to emergency management. Focuses on water justice, civil rights, land, education. During 2014 water shutoffs, organized volunteer water deliveries. Used community research to document socioeconomic consequences of austerity. [Source: We the People of Detroit, https://www.wethepeopleofdetroit.com/who-we-are]
- **Detroit People's Platform (DPP)**: Founded 2013, united local organizations for racial and economic justice in the nation's largest majority-Black city. Centers Black communities, confronting anti-Black racism as a pathway to dismantling systemic racism. [Source: Detroit People's Platform, https://www.detroitpeoplesplatform.org/about/]
- **Grace Lee Boggs**: (Not found in search but important context) Legendary Detroit activist (1915-2015), philosopher of revolution, co-founder of Detroit Summer, advocate for community-based transformation. Her work directly inspired community organizing in Detroit for decades. This is widely documented in her autobiography "Living for Change" and in "The Next American Revolution: Sustainable Activism for the Twenty-First Century."
- **Game Mechanic**: Community organizations should be named NPCs or faction groups that propose projects, respond to events, and shift political dynamics. They are not just "meters" -- they are actors.

### 4.6 Labor History and Unions

- **UAW Founding**: United Auto Workers founded August 26, 1935, in Detroit. Pioneered sit-down strike tactics. [Source: MotorCities, "The Rise of the United Auto Workers Union," https://www.motorcities.org/southwest-detroit-auto-heritage-guide/the-rise-of-the-united-auto-workers-union]
- **Key Victories**: 44-day sit-down strike in Flint (1936-37) forced GM to recognize the UAW. Under Walter Reuther (president 1946-1970), established unemployment benefits, wage adjustments, health plans, and vacations -- agreements that set the base for industrial unions nationwide. [Source: Britannica, "United Automobile Workers," https://www.britannica.com/topic/United-Automobile-Workers]
- **Broader Detroit Labor**: Detroit was the cradle of American labor rights. The labor movement shaped everything from working conditions to civil rights (UAW supported the 1963 March on Washington).
- **Game Mechanic**: Worker cooperatives, labor organizing events, tension between union history and new economy models. Auto workers as a constituency with specific concerns about industrial transition.

### 4.7 Environmental Justice Organizations

- **Detroiters Working for Environmental Justice (DWEJ)**: Founded 1994, Michigan's first environmental justice organization. Programs include Build Up Detroit, Green Jobs Training, Youth on Patrol Against Pollution. Focuses on asthma, lead poisoning, cardiovascular disease disparities. [Source: DWEJ, https://detroitenvironmentaljustice.org/about-us/]
- **Zero Waste Detroit (ZWD)**: Coalition of 20+ organizations since 2007, working toward recycling-based solid waste management and opposing waste incineration. Note: curbside recycling requires households to purchase their own $25 cart -- unaffordable for many Detroit families. [Source: Michigan Environmental Council, "Zero Waste Detroit," https://environmentalcouncil.org/members/zero-waste-detroit/]
- **Game Mechanic**: Environmental justice organizations as allies who propose projects, provide expertise, and mobilize community support. Waste management as a policy decision with equity implications.

## 5. Cahokia and Indigenous Context

### 5.1 Cahokia at Its Peak (~1100 CE)

- **Population**: Between 10,000 and 20,000 people at its peak (1050-1150 CE). Larger than London at the same time. [Source: Live Science, "Cahokia: North America's First City," https://www.livescience.com/22737-cahokia.html]
- **Urban Structure**: Covered ~6 square miles with ~120 earthworks. Meticulously planned around a 50-acre Grand Plaza. Featured well-structured roads, neighborhoods, defensive walls, and a functional water management system. [Source: Wikipedia, "Cahokia," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cahokia]
- **Monks Mound**: The largest prehistoric earthwork in the Americas north of Mexico. 100 feet high, covers 14 acres at its base (roughly the same footprint as the Great Pyramid of Giza). Built entirely by hand using baskets to transport ~22 million cubic feet of earth. [Source: The Archaeologist, "The Cahokia Mounds," https://www.thearchaeologist.org/blog/the-cahokia-mounds-the-rise-and-fall-of-a-mississippi-metropolis]
- **Political Structure**: During the Stirling phase (1100-1200 CE), Cahokia was at its height of political centralization. It was the largest and most influential urban settlement of the Mississippian culture. [Source: EBSCO, "Cahokia Becomes the First North American City," https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/anthropology/cahokia-becomes-first-north-american-city]

### 5.2 The "Voluntary Dispersal" Interpretation

- **What Happened**: Rather than a catastrophic collapse, environmental and political pressures compounded over roughly two centuries, driving wave after wave of emigration until no one remained. People left in what appears to be a sustained, voluntary exodus. [Source: Washington University, "New study adds to mystery of Cahokia exodus," https://artsci.washu.edu/ampersand/new-study-adds-mystery-cahokia-exodus]
- **Challenging Drought Theory**: Recent research (2024) found no evidence that prairie grasses were taking over, which would be expected in widespread crop failure. Carbon isotope ratios stayed relatively consistent. This suggests departure was not driven solely by agricultural collapse. [Source: Heritage Daily, "Study challenges the narrative of Cahokia's abandonment," https://www.heritagedaily.com/2024/07/study-challenges-the-narrative-of-cahokias-abandonment/152521]
- **Scholar Interpretation**: "I don't envision a scene where thousands of people were suddenly streaming out of town. People probably just spread out to be near kin or to find different opportunities." Many went south to the Cairo Lowlands, the Central Mississippi Valley, and the Cumberland Basin. [Source: National Geographic, "Why was the ancient city of Cahokia abandoned?," https://www.nationalgeographic.com/environment/article/why-was-ancient-city-of-cahokia-abandoned-new-clues-rule-out-one-theory]
- **Game Mechanic**: This is the game's philosophical core. Cahokia's people didn't "fail" -- they recognized the costs of centralized urbanism and chose to reorganize. The game should include moments where the player confronts the costs of growth and chooses de-growth or dispersal. The "Beyond the Map" stage should reflect this: Detroit becomes one node in a network, not a centralizing force.

### 5.3 Mississippian Land Management Practices

- **Agriculture**: Three Sisters (corn, beans, squash) intercropping as the foundation. Corn as a trellis for beans; beans fix nitrogen; squash leaves shade soil, retain moisture, prevent weeds. Also grew sunflowers, goosefoot, sumpweed. [Source: Moundville, "Mississippian Cuisine," https://www.moundville.org/mississippian-cuisine-learn-from-ancient-agriculture/]
- **Ecological Integration**: Mississippians lived close to rivers where periodic flooding replenished soil nutrients. They used intercropping and crop rotation to maintain soil productivity across generations. Proximity to productive fields, aquatic habitats, and wetlands was essential to settlement planning. [Source: Illinois State Museum, "Mississippian: Environment," https://www.museum.state.il.us/RiverWeb/landings/Ambot/Archives/History/Cahokia/miss/econ.html]
- **Tools and Methods**: Used stone axes, digging sticks, and fire. Mill Creek chert hoes were key tools distributed through trade networks. [Source: UW-La Crosse, "Mississippian Farmers," https://www.uwlax.edu/mvac/pre-european-people/mississippian--oneota-traditions/mississippian-farmers/]
- **Game Mechanic**: Three Sisters agriculture as a project type that provides food sovereignty + ecological health + soil restoration simultaneously. Adjacency to water tiles should provide flooding/nutrient bonuses, reflecting Mississippian settlement patterns.

### 5.4 Three Sisters Agriculture and Food Sovereignty

- **The System**: Corn, beans, and squash -- companion planting where each supports the others. Originated in Mesoamerica; squash domesticated first (~10,000 years ago), followed by maize and beans over 5,000-6,500 years. [Source: Wikipedia, "Three Sisters (agriculture)," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Sisters_(agriculture)]
- **Food Sovereignty Significance**: Native growers underscore the importance of rejuvenating indigenous agricultural practices as a path toward food sovereignty -- "the right of communities to shape food policy" and "to eat healthy, nutritious, and culturally valued foods acquired in culturally appropriate ways." [Source: Indigenous Climate Hub, "The Three Sisters as Indigenous Sustainable Agricultural Practice," https://indigenousclimatehub.ca/2023/06/the-three-sisters-as-indigenous-sustainable-agricultural-practice/]
- **Environmental Stewardship**: Three Sisters supports growth without requiring fertilizers, pesticides, equipment, or intense irrigation. It is a form of environmental stewardship -- responsible use and protection of the environment. [Source: PMC, "Reuniting the Three Sisters," https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9288846/]
- **Game Mechanic**: Three Sisters garden as a specific project type with unique benefits: no fertilizer input needed, provides food sovereignty + soil health + biodiversity. Should be available early as a low-cost, high-benefit option. Connects to indigenous knowledge systems.

### 5.5 Fire Management and Ecological Stewardship

- **Historical Practice**: Native Americans used fire to clear areas for crops and travel, manage land for specific species, and hunt game. Fire promoted ecological diversity and reduced catastrophic wildfire risk. [Source: NPS, "Indigenous Fire Practices Shape our Land," https://www.nps.gov/subjects/fire/indigenous-fire-practices-shape-our-land.htm]
- **Cultural Burning**: "Intentional lighting of smaller, controlled fires to provide a desired cultural service" -- promoting health of vegetation and animals for food, clothing, ceremonial items. Small, frequent, low-intensity burns during cooler/wetter periods. [Source: University of California, "How the Indigenous practice of 'good fire' can help our forests thrive," https://www.universityofcalifornia.edu/news/how-indigenous-practice-good-fire-can-help-our-forests-thrive]
- **Modern Revival**: US Forest Service works with tribes (Yurok, Karuk in California) to apply traditional burning techniques that reduce wildfire risk and increase availability of plants for basket weaving -- restoring both ecological health and cultural traditions. [Source: Nature Conservancy, "Indigenous Tribes Restore Prescribed Burns in California," https://www.nature.org/en-us/magazine/magazine-articles/indigenous-controlled-burns-california/]
- **Game Mechanic**: Prescribed burn as a project type for ecological restoration -- improves prairie health, reduces wildfire risk, promotes native plant growth. Connects to indigenous knowledge systems.

### 5.6 Authentic Representation Without Appropriation

- **Key Principle**: Indigenous knowledge should be presented as living traditions maintained by contemporary indigenous communities, not as "ancient wisdom" extracted for non-indigenous use.
- **Approach**: Feature indigenous-led organizations as in-game allies and knowledge holders. Present Three Sisters agriculture and fire management as indigenous innovations that can be practiced with proper acknowledgment. Avoid using indigenous cultural elements as mere aesthetic decorations.
- **Language**: Use the Anishinaabe name Waawiyatanong alongside Detroit. Acknowledge the Three Fires Council as the original stewards of the land.

### 5.7 Indigenous Peoples of the Detroit Area

- **The Three Fires Confederacy**: Waawiyatanong (Detroit) has been a central meeting place for the Anishinaabe: Ojibwe, Odawa, and Potawatomi. The Three Fires Council was established at Michilimackinac in 796 AD. [Source: Geez Magazine, "Waawiyatanong: A Detroit Land Acknowledgement," https://geezmagazine.org/magazine/article/waawiyatanong-land-acknowledgement]
- **Original Name**: The Detroit Indigenous Peoples Alliance passed a resolution in City Council to rename Detroit "Waawiyatanong," a word that connects the place to the water. [Source: same]
- **Land Cessions**: Through the Treaty of Detroit (1807), the Ojibwe, Ottawa, Potawatomi, and Wyandot tribes ceded the land now occupied by the city. Major displacement began with the Treaty of 1836. [Source: NPS, "Native American History in Detroit," https://www.nps.gov/articles/000/native-american-history-in-detroit.htm]
- **Seasonal Living**: In the 1600s, the Anishinaabe lived in different places for each season, depending on available natural resources -- hunting, planting, gathering, and fishing. [Source: WDET, "CuriosiD: Who Were the Natives in Detroit?," https://wdet.org/2017/04/30/curiosid-who-were-the-natives-in-detroit/]

### 5.8 Contemporary Indigenous-Led Stewardship

- **Detroit Sugarbush Project**: Antonio Rafael and shakara tyler of Detroit, with David Pitawanakwat of Wikwemikong First Nation, revived the Anishinaabe tradition of maple sugar harvesting in Rouge Park. The project also restores ecosystems through eliminating invasive species, planting native trees, cleaning trash, and spreading seeds. [Source: PBS News, "Activists tap a sweet Indigenous tradition to connect youth of color in Detroit with the outdoors," https://www.pbs.org/newshour/education/activists-tap-a-sweet-indigenous-tradition-to-connect-youth-of-color-in-detroit-with-the-outdoors]
- **Great Lakes Intertribal Food Coalition (GLIFC)**: Demonstrates benefits of food systems centered on responsibility to community and earth. Many Great Lakes tribes have food sovereignty programs with community gardens, food processing classes, and harvesting events. [Source: Frontiers, "The Great Lakes Intertribal Food Coalition," https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sustainable-food-systems/articles/10.3389/fsufs.2026.1816110/abstract]
- **Traditional Ecological Knowledge**: Tribes continue to lead in Great Lakes stewardship with unique place-based ecological knowledge critical to protecting these ecosystems. [Source: GLRI, "Distinct Tribal Program," https://www.glri.us/action-plan-iv/distinct-tribal-program]
- **Game Mechanic**: Indigenous-led restoration projects as a special project category. Partnership with tribal organizations as a community trust builder. The Detroit Sugarbush Project is a real model for a game project.

## 6. Solarpunk Reference Points

### 6.1 Existing Solarpunk Projects in Detroit

- **Solar Neighborhoods Initiative**: Detroit's citywide program transforming large vacant parcels into solar energy farms. Five utility-scale solar arrays to power 127 municipal buildings. Homeowners within each footprint receive $15,000-$25,000 in free home energy upgrades. Reduces heat-island temperatures and improves air quality. [Source: City of Detroit, "Solar Neighborhoods," https://detroitmi.gov/government/mayors-office/office-sustainability/energy/solar-neighborhoods]
- **Soulardarity (Highland Park)**: Community-owned solar streetlights replacing utility-repossessed infrastructure. National model for energy democracy. [Source: Vice, "This Off-Grid Project Is Bringing Streetlights Back to Detroit," https://www.vice.com/en/article/this-off-grid-project-is-bringing-streetlights-back-to-detroit/]
- **North End Community Solar Trust**: $200,000 DOE grant enabling community solar arrays at key sites in Detroit's North End, Highland Park, and Hamtramck. Energy credits redistributed to local residents. [Source: Model D, "Detroit has community buy-in for Solar Neighborhoods Initiative," https://modeldmedia.com/detroit-has-community-buy-in-for-solar-neighborhoods-initiative/]
- **Brownfield Solar**: Michigan exploring solar installations on contaminated brownfield land -- combining remediation with renewable energy. [Source: Planet Detroit, "Can solar projects transform Michigan's contaminated land?," https://planetdetroit.org/2025/05/michigan-brownfields-solar-energy/]
- **Community Land Trusts**: Detroit Cultivator, Dream of Detroit, Common Grounds CLT, Avalon Village -- establishing permanent community ownership of land. [Source: Outlier Media, https://outliermedia.org/detroit-community-land-trusts/]
- **True North Detroit**: Quonset steel hut micro-community in Core City with gardens, community pavilion, and pathways. [Source: Detroitisit, https://detroitisit.com/core-city-what-is-it-why-is-it-where-is-it-whos-in-it/]

### 6.2 Highway Removal Examples

- **Detroit's Own I-375**: The $104.6 million project to remove the shortest highway in the US, which destroyed the Black Bottom and Paradise Valley neighborhoods when built in 1964. Displaced ~100,000 Black residents and 2,500+ Black-owned businesses. Current plans: 6-lane boulevard with wide sidewalks and bike lanes; 25-31 acres freed for development. Community groups want fewer lanes, reparative justice, and community benefits. Construction slated for 2025. [Source: Washington Post, "Detroit wins $100 million to remove I-375," https://www.washingtonpost.com/transportation/2022/09/15/detroit-highway-removal-paradise-valley/]
- **San Francisco's Octavia Boulevard** (2003): Replaced the earthquake-damaged Central Freeway. [Source: Lincoln Institute, "Deconstruction Ahead," https://www.lincolninst.edu/publications/articles/2020-03-deconstruction-ahead-urban-highway-removal-changing-cities/]
- **Oakland's Mandela Parkway**: Replaced highway with boulevard, reducing nitrogen oxide by 38% and black carbon by 25%. New community focal point promoting connected, active urban life. [Source: APM Research Lab, "Highway removal: Reconnecting neighborhoods," https://www.apmresearchlab.org/10x/highway-removal-reconnecting-neighborhoods-and-the-natural-environment]
- **Milwaukee Park East**: $25 million removal freed 24 acres, yielding $1 billion in new private investment. [Source: CNU, "Eight completed highway removals," https://www.cnu.org/publicsquare/2022/05/31/eight-completed-highway-removals-tell-story-movement]
- **Federal Program**: Reconnecting Communities and Neighborhoods Grant Program ($185 million in 2023) -- first-of-its-kind initiative to reconnect communities cut off by past infrastructure decisions. [Source: Urban Land Magazine, "Highway Removals," https://urbanland.uli.org/sustainability/highway-removals-communities-reunite-breaking-down-barriers-and-righting-wrongs-of-the-past]
- **Game Mechanic**: Highway removal as a major multi-turn project. The I-375 removal should be a signature event/project in the game. Removing highways reconnects neighborhoods, unlocks development land, and reduces pollution but requires massive budget and political will.

### 6.3 Urban Farms (Real Examples Beyond Detroit)

- Detroit has the most extensive urban farming movement of any US city
- Other notable examples: Growing Power (Milwaukee), South Central Farm (Los Angeles), Beacon Food Forest (Seattle)
- Detroit's 1,500+ gardens and farms are unmatched in scale

### 6.4 Water Transit

- **Detroit Water Taxi**: Operates scenic cruises and water taxi service on the Detroit River, including routes to Belle Isle and destinations along the riverfront. [Source: Detroit Water Taxi, https://detroitwatertaxi.org/]
- **Unrealized Potential**: $2.4 million in federal ferry funds sat unspent for 7+ years as of 2017. The Detroit port authority has not applied for federal ferry service grants from the $912 million available under the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law. [Source: Crain's Detroit Business, "$2.4 million for Detroit River ferries sits unspent," https://www.crainsdetroit.com/article/20170115/NEWS/170119890/2-4-million-for-detroit-river-ferries-sits-unspent-after-7-years]
- **Game Mechanic**: Water transit as a project type -- electrified ferries and water taxis connecting riverfront neighborhoods, Belle Isle, and potentially Canadian communities. Builds on existing but underutilized infrastructure.

### 6.5 Community Land Trusts

- Detroit is establishing multiple CLTs (see Section 2.13). The model permanently removes land from the speculative market, ensuring long-term affordability.
- **National Examples**: Champlain Housing Trust (Burlington, VT) -- largest CLT in the US, established 1984
- **Game Mechanic**: CLTs as a policy that prevents gentrification on transformed tiles. Once a CLT is established, the tile cannot be lost to market forces.

### 6.6 Maker Spaces

- **Cory Doctorow's "Makers" (2009)**: Novel about hardware hackers who repurpose devices and 3D-print products in a collapsing economy. Key concepts: distributed manufacturing networks, microinvesting in communal start-ups, the "New Work" as a New Deal for the technological era. Characterized makers as "people who hack hardware, business-models, and living arrangements to discover ways of staying alive and happy even when the economy is falling down the toilet." Available free under Creative Commons. [Source: Wikipedia, "Makers (novel)," https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makers_(novel)]
- **Real Detroit Maker Spaces**: Various maker spaces and artist workshops exist, particularly in Core City, Corktown, and Eastern Market areas.
- **Game Mechanic**: Maker spaces as a project type that provides economic activity without extractive industry. Connects to the cooperative economics theme of the Transition stage.

### 6.7 Cities That Have "De-Grown" Intentionally

- **"Smart Decline"**: Planning for less -- fewer people, fewer buildings, fewer land uses -- as a development method focused on improving quality of life for current residents. [Source: Shrinking city, Wikipedia, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shrinking_city]
- **European Examples**: Turin (lost 25% of population, 130,000+ manufacturing jobs since 1970s); Frankfurt-on-Oder (population dropped from 88,000 to 58,000 post-reunification); Hoyerswerda (youngest population in East Germany in 1980s, oldest by 2000s). [Source: The Conversation, "The challenges of degrowth in cities," https://theconversation.com/the-challenges-of-degrowth-in-cities-110867]
- **Managed Approaches**: Genoa pursuing policies to reduce urban fabric and decrease density in areas with demographic decline, pollution, or catastrophic risk. Parkstad Limburg (Netherlands) exploring urban greening in shrinking contexts. Genesee County Land Bank (Flint, MI) as a model for managing vacant properties. [Source: Cogitatio Press, "The Shrinking City as a Testing Ground for Urban Degrowth Practices," https://www.cogitatiopress.com/urbanplanning/article/view/8008]
- **Detroit Future City**: The DFC framework plan (2012) is essentially a managed degrowth document -- proposing green uses for vast vacant land rather than trying to rebuild to 1950s population levels. [Source: Detroit Future City, https://detroitfuturecity.com/our-programs/]
- **Game Mechanic**: The game itself is about de-growth/transformation rather than growth. This should be the explicit framing: you are not rebuilding Detroit to 1.85 million people. You are reimagining what a city looks like with 640,000 people and 24 square miles of open land.

## Key Themes for Game Authenticity

### What Makes Detroit Unique for This Game

1. **Scale of vacancy**: 19-24 square miles of vacant land is not a problem -- it's an unprecedented opportunity for urban ecological transformation.
2. **Water wealth**: Sitting on one-fifth of the world's fresh surface water while residents can't afford water bills is the defining paradox.
3. **Community organizing tradition**: Detroit has some of the strongest grassroots organizations in the US, forged through crisis.
4. **Racial justice centrality**: Detroit is ~78% Black. Every system in the game must grapple with racial equity.
5. **Already happening**: Unlike many "solarpunk" visions, Detroit already has urban farms, community solar, land trusts, and river restoration underway. The game depicts acceleration of real trends.
6. **Indigenous foundation**: Waawiyatanong is Anishinaabe land. Ecological restoration connects to indigenous stewardship that predates industrial destruction.
